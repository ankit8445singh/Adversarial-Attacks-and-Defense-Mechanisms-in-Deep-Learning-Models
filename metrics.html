Applied ML Findings

The Accuracy vs. Robustness Trade-off

As shown in Chart 1, the clean accuracy (at ε = 0) of the adversarially trained CNN is slightly lower (82.0%) compared to the standard model (91.0%). This gap of ~9% represents the robust regularization cost. In training a model to minimize worst-case adversarial loss, the decision boundary is smoothed, which can cause slight errors on standard inputs. However, under attack, the standard model accuracy drops catastrophically to 22% at ε = 0.15, whereas the robust model retains a highly stable 67% accuracy.

Defensive Preprocessing Limitations

Input preprocessing defenses (Chart 2) like JPEG compression and bit-depth quantization are computationally cheap because they require no expensive retraining. They act as high-frequency noise eliminators, recovering model accuracy up to ~60% under moderate attacks. However, their defense curves decay much faster than Adversarial Training under larger perturbations. Furthermore, they are vulnerable to Adaptive Attacks, where the attacker incorporates the preprocessor's gradients directly into the optimization.

Research Conclusions Summary:

  1. No Free Lunch: Improving worst-case adversarial robustness inevitably shifts classification boundaries, causing a minor tax on standard prediction scores.
  2. Information Destructive Defenses: Spatial average smoothing and bit quantization remove noise but also destroy critical object edge shapes, explaining why their standard clean accuracy is lower.
  3. Multi-tier Defense: A comprehensive security architecture should combine **Adversarial Training** as the core model backbone with **Input Compression filters** to mitigate unknown zero-day high-frequency noise profiles.
